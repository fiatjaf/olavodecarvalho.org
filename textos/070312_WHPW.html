Leading Brazilian conservative intellectual talks with WHPW
(Part I)

Western Hemisphere Policy Watch, March 12th, 2007

 

 

Esta é a primeira parte da entrevista que concedi a Western Hemisphere Policy Watch, um site lido diariamente pela elite política de Washington. A entrevistadora quis permanecer anônima, mas agradeço a ela a ajuda inestimável que prestou à divulgação de fatos que a grande mídia, brasileira ou americana, teima em ignorar. – O. de C.

Olavo de Carvalho, 59, is a Brazilian writer and philosopher presently living in the U.S. as a foreign correspondent for Brazilian newspapers.

In an exclusive interview for Western Hemisphere Policy Watch (WHPW), the former university lecturer on Political Philosophy in Sao Paulo, Rio de Janeiro and Curitiba, discusses a wide-array of topics, the most perplexing of which is the legal battle being waged by Brazilian communists against him and his works.

Carvalho is the author of a dozen books and a widely read weekly columnist in Brazilian press. He also was a fan and supporter of former Reagan Administration official, the late Dr. Constantine Menges – a favorite of WHPW Editors. We would like to publicly thank folks who made this possible, but our correspondent wants to remain anonymous.

Given the length of the interview, we shall run it in two parts. Today, Carvalho (ODC) generally dicusses the state of Brazilian media and freedom of speech. What he says will surprise you. The second part of the interview discussing his personal battle against Brazilian state censorship against conservative view points shall be run later this week.

Let us know what you think, HMJ.

***

WHPW: What is the status of free and open media in Brazil today?

ODC: There is no official censorship in Brazil because there is no real need for it. Since the 1960s the Left launched a huge campaign to occupy all the spaces and to dislodge its enemies from news offices in Brazilian newspapers and TV channels.

There are virtually no conservatives in the Brazilian media. The few remaining ones keep their mouths shut not to lose their jobs. The political debate in Brazilian newspapers and TV channels takes place only between different leftist groups.

Even in the Parliament, most of what is today called the “opposition” is constituted only by social-democrats, a moderate leftwing party. The so-called “rightwing party” – the PFL (Party of the Liberal Front) – defines itself in the tradition of the American Democratic Party.

There is no Brazilian equivalent to the GOP or to the British Conservative Party. Can you imagine a democracy where the only active forces were the Democratic Party and the Communist Party? This is Brazil.

WHPW: Why is the media manipulated? Who is responsible for manipulating media today?

ODC: “… most of the media are awfully indebted to the official banks. The government uses its financial power to intimidate and to control the press. But it finds no difficulty to do so, for the companies themselves are willfully submissive. Even when they criticize the government, they do so only in minor details, avoiding any mention to the Sao Paulo Forum or Brazilian President Lula da Silva’s links to the Colombian narco-guerillas.

Everybody knows that Mr. Boris Casoy, the successful anchor of Bandeirantes TV Channel, who mentioned the forbidden matters in his TV commentaries, was fired under government pressure. As for myself, I lost my jobs in three newspapers and one weekly magazine because of my insistence in denouncing the Sao Paulo Forum.

In cultural matters, the leftist hegemony is so extended that even the few media that are hostile to the government, like the weekly magazine Veja, are supportive of the left in every moral or religious controversy, besides flattering the leftist intellectuals and boycotting the conservative ones.

WHPW: What will it take to help Brazilians not feel so afraid and overcome by state-controls?

ODC: If only the American media told Americans what is really going on in Brazil, everything would be easier for us.

But American journalists seem to look at the facts only from the economic side. They see Lula running a capitalist economy and they conclude that he is America’s friend. But there is no substantial contradiction between running a capitalist economy while draining its energies to feed a growing communist movement and to support the arrangements for a continental "war of the whole people" against the USA, as planned by Hugo Chavez.

Lula's government can be defined, alas, by that two-headed strategy wisely grounded on the mutual independence between economy and conspiracy. Many Brazilian businessmen let themselves to be guided by the big media, both Brazilian and American. As they find no news about the Sao Paulo Forum in the big newspapers from both Brazil and USA, they will never understand the real state of affairs in Brazil.

Only in the State of São Paulo a large part of the businessmen are acquainted to the facts, owing to the Sao Paulo Chamber of Commerce, a brave conservative organization lead by the former presidential candidate Guilherme Afif Domingos. In every other Brazilian state the businessmen are quite mistaken about the government’s political alliances with the continental Left.
