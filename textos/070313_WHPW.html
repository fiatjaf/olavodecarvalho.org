Leading Brazilian conservative intellectual talks with WHPW
(Part II)

Western Hemisphere Policy Watch, March 13th, 2007

 

(From the Banks of the Potomac) Today we bring you Part II of III in this series with Dr. Olavo de Carvalho, but our correspondent wants to remain anonymous. We thought we could release the entire interview in two parts, but upon review, we decided it was best to break it down into three segments.

Today, we are publishing details on the several legal attacks on Carvalho, his work, and that of the Brazilian conservative movement. Read Part I, here. We especially note this comment about the U.S.: "They [Americans] don't understand how easy it is to use capitalist success to feed the power of communist and pro-communist organizations. This is really what Lula is doing. They are not acquainted with Lula's deep and unflinching loyalty to the organizations that have been supporting him all along his political career."

For authentication purposes, we have asked Dr. Carvalho to link our posts to his site, here (we know there are a few of you out here in town who will undoubtedly e-mail us for some confirmation that it was, indeed, Carvalho's text below).

In Part III and final segment of this interview, Carvalho will detail some interesting facts about certain pro-leftist officials in Brazil's military.

Let us know what you think, HMJ.

***

WHPW: Tell our readers a little more about the law suit against you.

OC: There are really four suits. The first one was filed by the federal prosecutor itself, soon after I had published in O Globo an article called “Drink, Mr. President.” It was really not an aggressive article. In it I stated that I didn’t know if the New York Times correspondent Harry Rother was right in calling Lula a drunk, but that if I were Lula I would drink a lot in order to avoid facing his disgusting situation.

On one hand he [Lula] had promised to put an end to criminal violence, on the other he had signed a solemn commitment to support the FARC, the Colombian narcoguerrillas who were among the main culprits of violence in Brazil. Isn't it a good reason to get drunk?

The suit was filed by the attorney-general office in november 2004, under the number 2004.51.60.017221-4 in the city of Sao Paolo do Meriti, Rio de Janeiro, where the main office of O Globo is located. But as I moved to the U.S. in May 2005 (I didn't know about the suit then), the subpoena didn't reach me in due time. When the judicial agents at last found my address here, it was too late. The suit became void in november 2006.

WHPW: What about the other law suits?

OC: The second suit is Case n. 583.00.2006.138856-6 of the 1st Court of Sao Paulo.

In an article published in Madia Sem Ma¡scara (Media Unmasked), our columnist Ipojuca Pontes demonstrated how Roberto Teixeira, a close friend to President Lula and godfather of one of his sons, used his influence to raise public money from many cities governed by the Worker's Party (PT, Partido dos Trabalhadores, Brazil's governing party).

According to several investigations, the money wasn't registered in PT's bookeeping and presumably had been used to consolidate the party's political power in Brazil. Though the case was published by several newspapers, Mr. Teixeira chose to file a lawsuit selectively only against our columnist, demanding him to pay compensation for moral damage.

The third case was a judicial order (I don't have the number and date here) filed by the federal representative Lula Eduardo Greenhalgh against my electronic newspaper to remove from our website some articles he didn't like.

Mr. Greenhalgh was acting as a lawyer for some Worker's Party politicians who were accused of corruption. One of them was accused also of being an accomplice in the murder of the mayor if the city of Santo Andre, Celso Daniel, himself a WP's militant but an honest man who was collecting evidences in order to denounce his corrupt colleagues. As Daniel was a homosexual, the respondents tried to explore this in order to dismiss the case as a crime of passion. But soon afterwards no less than six witnesses for the prosecution were also assassinated, as well as the coroner that had examined Daniel's corpse.

WHPW: What happened next?

OC: A little later a police wiretap surprised Mr. Greenhalgh telling the presidential spokesman Gilberto Carvalho (no relation to me) that they should try to manoeuver someway the next witness, Celso Daniel's brother Francisco Daniel, in order to stop him from telling the court, as he intended to do, that the case was a political murder planned by the Worker's Party to shut up a man who knew too much.

The conversation itself was strong evidence that Francisco Daniel was right. Madia Sem Ma¡scara reported this news, but every newspaper in Brazil was reported on it. So why did Mr Greenhalgh chose to sue only us?

As Mídia Sem Máscara is the only overtly conservative and anticommunist media in Brazil, and a very small and defenseless one at that, the political discrimination is evident. Significantly enough, the same article (written by me) that allegedly motivated Mr. Greenhalgh's complaint was published simultaneosly by MSM and by the Diario do Comercio(Business Daily), the newspaper belonging to the Sao Paulo Chamber of Commerce, but Mr. Greenhalgh sued only MSM, not the Diario.

Of course. He is not crazy. It's easier to fight a dozen penniless conservative writers than a bunch of the business big shots in Sao Paulo. Luiz Eduardo Greenhalg used a subpoena to warn my electronic newspaper to avoid publishing stories on his political activism as, for example, his appeal for a violent toppling of the 1988 Constitutional Government and for the dissolution of the Brazilian Army.

WHPW: Tell us about the last suit. This one really appears interesting, especially how it indirectly, and not so indirectly, impacts many good officials in the Brazilian military.

OC: The fourth suit is the most original of all. The case is number 2007.800.005199-2, filed at the 17th Special Court of Rio de Janeiro.

One of the main propaganda topics of the Brazilian Left is to demoralize the military as responsible for violence and tortures practiced against leftist militants during the Brazilian dictatorship (1964-1984). Some of the accusations are genuine, but many are very controversial. All of them, however, are officially accepted as the final truth as soon as they are published by the media.

The news is then used as evidence in claim for federal compensations for the self-proclaimed victims of the repressive state, most of them former terrorists, while the victims of their terrorist acts, and their families, receive nothing at all and are seldom if ever mentioned by the media.

Among the military officers accused of torture is Coronel Alberto Brilhante Ustra (this sounds Brill-yantee Oostra). For years he has been trying to defend himself, but the media was not paying attention to him, only to his accusers. He then wrote a book, A Verdade Sufocada (A Suffocated Truth), to tell his version of the facts. Suprisingly enough, the book was an astounding success.

WHPW: Tell us some more about the book and what has happened since.

OC: At first reluctantly, bit by bit the big booksellers started to sell it and now you can find it everywhere in Brazil. It happened that, Colonel Ustra's website, www.averdadesufocada.com, mistakingly quoted as a favorable reviewer of the book a lawyer and journalist, Adilson Marcos da Silva, who was instead one the the real characters of the story.

Mr. Marcos, a rabid leftist and anti-Antiamerican trumpeter, got angry with the Colonel and reasonably so, but decided to take revenge against an innocent: my editor, partner and representative in Brazil, Edson Oliveira.

First, Mr. Marcos arbitrarily picked six bookshops among the hundreds that were selling the book, and then he sued Mr. Oliveira as being responsible for all of them, which he is not. Mr. Oliveira owns only one bookshop and publishing house, he is not the publisher of Colonel Ustra's book and he cannot be held responsible for a mistake committed by the Colonel. If he is now summoned to pay compensations to Mr. Marcos, his real guilt lies elsewhere: the main item in his catalogue are my books and the DVDs of my lectures, most of them recorded at the company's auditorium.

WHPW: What do you expect to be the outcome?

OC: It's impossible to say. Anything can happen. The judicial system in Brazil is changing very fast due to political interference. Many of the old judges have been replaced by political activists affiliated to a movement called alternative Law, which overtly professes to judge rule to the ideological meaning of each case and not the written Law.

If the cases are brought to normal judges, everything will be OK. But if we fall into the claws of alternative Law judges, we are lost. As judges for for each case are chosen at random, it all depends on luck.

WHPW: Why does Lula tolerate these matters and people

OC: Mr. Teixeira and Mr. Greenhalgh are his old friends and correligionists. He would never leave them unassisted. People here in the U.S. are easily impressed by the Brazilian governmen's political economy, which is really pro-capitalism. They don't understand how easy it is to use capitalist success to feed the power of communist and pro-communist organizations. This is really what Lula is doing. They are not acquainted with Lula's deep and unflinching loyalty to the organizations that have been supporting him all along his political career.
